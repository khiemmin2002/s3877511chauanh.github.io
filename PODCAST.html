PODCAST

This is a young podcast, discussing Vietnamese teen stress. Nguyen Nha Uyen is the host of the program, the guests are Minh Anh - Rector of Ngo Quyen High School, Gia Khanh - a single father and Chau Anh - Psychologist.

RECORDING

YOUNG&WILDPODCAST- MENTAL HEALTH SOS

    [Intro Music] -  Better Days (Tissot n.d.) 

Host:Hello everyone, my name is Uyen! Welcome back to Young Wild Podcast. Today, we will talk about the “stress” issue of Vietnamese teenagers. Please welcome our 2 guests today: Mrs Minh Anh- The principal of Ngo Quyen high school.

    [Hand clapping sound] -  Audience Applause (Matthiew11 2015)   

Guest 1:Hi everyone, thank you for inviting me to the show today.   
Host:And Gia Khanh- A single dad of a teenager kid

    [Hand clapping sound] -  Audience Applause (Matthiew11 2015) 
  
Guest 2:  Well, well!It's great to be here and share about teenager's mentality based on my experience.   

Host: Ok, first, let me give some background information: “Stress is regarded as a form of uncertainty” (Peters, McEwen, Friston 2017). Everything changes when you become a teenager, they often behave in an unexpected way(Fox 2020). Jeanne Meijs (2013, p.13)- said that “Teenagehood is a narrow path for growing children”.   

Guest 2:Uhmm, I do care about teenagers, because my daughter actually belongs to this range of ages. I really wonder how many Vietnamese teenagers are having stress problems nowadays?   

Host:  According to a study by UNICEF, about 8% - 29% of adolescents in Vietnam suffering from stress problems (UNICEF, cited in VINMEC 2020). Well, I'm gonna give the mic to Mrs Minh Anh. She will share about the reasons for this problem!    

Guest 1:  UHMM, as I know, becoming under pressure from parents is the main reason. According to Christie Blazer: “Many experts maintain that parents’ unrealistically high expectations create pressure and foster performance anxiety in their children” (Sedere, McClure, Ramírez, Cech, Clemmitt, Pascucci, Pomfret, Johns Hopkins University Center for Talented Youth, cited in Christie Blazer 2010). Parents always create pressure for their children and don’t care about their feelings. As usual, they just tell their children to do and get that result, otherwise they will be scolded.      
        
Host: Thanks Minh Anh. Khanh, will this reason lead to any effect?

 Absolutely! When the parents try to force their children to get the achievement they want, their children potentially experience constant anxiety. Accordingly, their children are inclined to get many risks of mental health (Amy 2020). Alarmingly, when getting mental problems, youngsters are likely to kill themselves (KidsHealth 2020). There is a fascinating point that parents are potential elements that contribute to their children’s suicide, when they put too much out-of-reach expectation on their children (UNICEF 2017).   

Host: Thank you for your sharing!  We still have a special guest. I will call her right now!

    [Phone ringing sound] -  Ringing Phone (Koenig 2011) 

Host: Welcome our expert - Chau Anh to the show today! 

    [Hand clapping sound] -  Audience Applause (Matthiew11 2015) 

Expert: Hello everyone, it is my pleasure to be here! 

Host:So, in case of parents over expectation to their children, do you have any advice? 

Expert:  In my opinion, parents need to learn how to sympathize with their children. To achieve this goal, parents should patiently listen and talk to children. They should encourage their children and build a relationship, so that their children will feel comfortable when asking for help (University of Wisconsin, Garrett, Copeland, Wilde, McCloud, Ebata, cited in Christie Blazer 2010) When children are open-minded in sharing their stress, parents will easily give their children advice as not only psychologists, but also as close friends.   

Host:Minh Anh, do you think there are any more factors that lead to stress among young children?   

Guest 1:In addition to the first reason, another one is because of the amount of homework. “Homework is often a source of student stress” (McClure, Suldo et al., Scott, Round Rock Independent School District, cited in Christie Blazer 2010). Outside of school, students often don’t have too much time to exercise and spend time with their friends or families. For instance, Vietnamese students have to study 13 subjects and do homework for all of those, so they don’t have enough time to finish and become depressed gradually.  

Host:  Khanh, please explain to me more about the effect of heavy course load?  

Guest 2:Sure! According to a University of California, getting stress early in life potentially brings about cognitive disorders in humans (HeartMath Institute 2014).  In other words, if the teenagers are under a lot of pressure with their education, they will easily suffer from cognitive disorders, which affect badly on memory, perception, and problem solving (Longdom 2020).    

Host:Let’s take a short break, and now a quick word from our sponsor.    

Advertisement: Hi, hi, hi!Welcome to Saigon Center Wellbeing! You are getting stress from your university course load? You do concern yourself, and your friends? Please feel free, come and join us at RMIT University. Our professional staff are here to help you - we do care about your physical, social, and emotional wellbeing. Don't be shy, take a try with our friendly team to get a better result in your university performance!

    [Soundtrack] – Inspire (Tissot n.d.)  

  Host:  Chau Anh, do you have any idea how to solve it? 

Expert:To reduce stress, students should learn stress management skills, such as relaxation techniques and problem-solving. Moreover, they should also learn coping skills, think positively, and take action toward a solution (Copeland, Scott, TeensHealth, Swiercinsky, cited in Christie Blazer 2010). As a result, students not only can reduce stress, but they also have a positive attitude toward studying, easily get the marks they want. 

Ending: Alright! Hope that everyone understands more about Vietnamese teenager stress issues and get some advice to deal with teenagers around you. And please do not forget to take a look at our magazine called “Mental health SOS” to understand more about this kind of sickness. Thanks everyone for listening.
